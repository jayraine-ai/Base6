🌊 Our Oceans

Why do oceans matter?

The ocean covers over 70% of our planet. It produces more than half the oxygen we breathe, regulates our climate, feeds billions of people, and is home to the most diverse range of life on Earth.

World Oceans Day is celebrated every year on 8th June — a day to appreciate, protect and think about our relationship with the sea.

The ocean isn't just where fish live. It's the lungs, the thermostat and the food bank of our entire planet.

🔗 Connection to our driving question

The ocean is one of the clearest examples of how choices made today shape the world of tomorrow. Decisions about plastic use, fishing, energy, and pollution all directly affect whether our oceans survive — and with them, life on Earth as we know it.

🤿 Dive Deep

How deep does the ocean go?

The ocean is divided into zones based on depth. The deeper you go, the darker, colder and more pressurised it becomes. Each zone is home to very different creatures. Click each zone to explore it.

☀️
Sunlight Zone
0 – 200m
▶
Most ocean life lives here. Sunlight reaches this zone, allowing plants and algae to grow. Coral reefs, dolphins, sharks, turtles and colourful fish all call this zone home. This is also where most human impact — from pollution, fishing and tourism — is felt.
🌅
Twilight Zone
200 – 1,000m
▶
Only faint light filters down here. Many creatures in this zone have large eyes to see in the dim light, or produce their own light (bioluminescence). The giant squid lives here. It's also home to some of the most bizarre-looking fish in the world.
🌑
Midnight Zone
1,000 – 4,000m
▶
Completely dark and freezing cold. The pressure here is crushing — equivalent to 400 atmospheres. Yet life still exists. Creatures like the anglerfish use their own glowing lures to attract prey in the pitch black. No sunlight, no plants, just hunters.
🌊
Abyssal Zone
4,000 – 6,000m
▶
The vast flat plains of the deep ocean floor. Near-freezing temperatures, complete darkness, and enormous pressure. Sea cucumbers, sea spiders and tiny crustaceans feed on particles that drift down from above — called marine snow. This zone covers more of Earth's surface than any other habitat.
🕳️
Hadal Zone
6,000m+
▶
The deepest ocean trenches — including the Mariana Trench at nearly 11km. Fewer humans have visited the deepest point on Earth than have walked on the moon. Yet even here, life exists. Scientists have found tiny snailfish, amphipods and microbial life — surviving in conditions that would destroy any human equipment.
