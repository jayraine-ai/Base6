⚠️ The Four Pitfalls of AI

AI isn't always right — and it doesn't always tell you that

AI tools like chatbots can be incredibly useful. But they have some serious weaknesses that you need to know about before you trust them. A well-designed AI should be honest about what it doesn't know. A poorly designed — or poorly used — AI can be dangerously misleading.

The most dangerous AI response isn't one that says "I don't know" — it's one that says the wrong thing with total confidence.

The four main pitfalls

❌
Confidently Wrong
Stating false information as if it were completely true — with no uncertainty
👻
Hallucination
Making up books, studies, quotes or sources that don't actually exist
🪞
Sycophancy
Agreeing with whatever the user says, even when the user is clearly wrong
👥
Bias
Producing answers that reflect unfair stereotypes about groups of people

🤔 Why does this happen?

AI models are trained on huge amounts of text from the internet. The internet contains a lot of misinformation, biased content, and outdated facts. AI models don't "know" things the way humans do — they predict what words are likely to come next based on patterns. That means they can generate text that sounds right but is completely wrong.

They also have a knowledge cutoff — a date after which they don't know what happened. And they have no way to check their own answers against reality unless they're specifically built to do so.

💭 Think Like a Detective

So what do we do about it?

Knowing about AI pitfalls isn't about being scared of AI — it's about being smart with it. A critical thinker doesn't just accept what any source tells them, whether that's a website, a book, a person, or an AI.

The skills you need to evaluate AI are the same ones you need to evaluate any source of information: ask questions, check sources, think about who benefits from the answer, and never stop being curious.

1. Which pitfall do you think is the most dangerous? Why?

Think about real-life situations where someone might be seriously misled.

2. Can you think of a real situation where an AI giving a confidently wrong answer could cause harm?

Think about medical advice, homework, news, legal advice, safety information...

3. What steps could you take to check whether an AI answer is trustworthy?

Think about what you already do when you're not sure if something is true.

4. Does knowing about these pitfalls change how you feel about using AI tools? How?
