How do we know if a source is telling the whole story?

What is Bias?

📖 History is written by people

Everything we know about the past comes from sources — things that were written, drawn, built or recorded. But every source was made by a person. And people have opinions, feelings and reasons for saying what they say.

When a source only tells part of the story, or twists the truth to favour one side, we call that bias.

Bias = when a source is unfairly influenced by the feelings, opinions or interests of the person who created it.

🤔 Why does bias happen?

People can be biased for lots of reasons. They might:

  • Want to make themselves or their country look good
  • Dislike or fear another group of people
  • Want to persuade others to agree with them
  • Only know one side of the story
  • Be writing for a particular audience

📰 A real example

During World War Two, governments on all sides produced propaganda — posters, films and leaflets designed to make their own side look heroic and the enemy look evil. This is bias on purpose.

But bias isn't always deliberate. A diary written by a soldier might only describe what they personally saw and felt — leaving out the bigger picture. That's bias too, even if the writer wasn't trying to deceive anyone.

All sources can be biased — even ones written by honest people. That's why historians always ask: who wrote this, and why?

🔍 Questions historians ask

  • Who created this source?
  • When was it created?
  • Why was it created?
  • Who was it created for?
  • What might be missing from this source?

🎮 Fact or Opinion?

What is the difference?

One way to spot bias is to look for opinions disguised as facts. A fact can be checked and proven. An opinion is what someone thinks or believes — even if they state it as if it were true.

Fact: "The Battle of Hastings took place in 1066."
Opinion: "The Norman invasion was good for Britain."
