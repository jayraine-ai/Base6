🗺️ The Three-Act Structure

Most films — even short ones — follow three acts. Try filling in each one for Box-Man.

Introduce Box-Man and his world. What is normal life like? What happens to kick off the story?

Things get harder. Box-Man faces his main enemy or obstacle. What goes wrong? What does he try?

The climax and ending. How does Box-Man solve the problem? What changes by the end?

✍️ Character Writing Tips

💪 Give them a want AND a need

What does your character think they want? What do they actually need? These are often different. Box-Man might want justice, but need to believe in himself.

😬 Give them a flaw

Perfect characters are boring. What is Box-Man bad at? What is his weakness? Flaws make characters feel real and give them somewhere to grow.

🗣️ Give them a unique voice

How does your character speak? Short punchy sentences? Long dramatic speeches? Jokes? Threats? The way a character talks tells us who they are.

🎭 Give them a backstory

You don't have to put it all in the film — but knowing WHY Box-Man delivers justice makes your writing richer. What happened to him in the past?

📖 How to Format a Script

Film scripts have a specific format. Here's what each part looks like — with a Box-Man example:

Example Script Format
INT. BOX-MAN'S WAREHOUSE — DAY

A dimly lit space. Cardboard boxes everywhere. BOX-MAN stands at the centre, adjusting his box-shaped helmet.

BOX-MAN
(to himself)
Another delivery. Another chance to make things right.

His phone BUZZES. He checks it — a message: "PACKAGE UNDELIVERED. LOCATION: UNKNOWN."

BOX-MAN
Not on my watch.

He grabs his delivery bag and STRIDES out into the light.
🎬 Scene Heading

INT. = inside. EXT. = outside. Then the LOCATION in capitals, then — DAY or — NIGHT.

🎭 Character Name

Before dialogue, write the character's name in CAPITALS, centred on the page.

💬 Dialogue

What the character says. Keep it punchy — in real films, less is more.

📹 Action Lines

Describe what we SEE on screen. Write in present tense: "Box-Man RUNS" not "Box-Man ran".

🗂️ Scene Planner

Plan each scene before you write it. What happens? Where? Who's in it?

✍️ Dialogue Tips

🎤 Say it out loud

The best test for dialogue is to read it aloud. If it sounds weird when you say it, change it. Real people don't speak in perfect sentences.

✂️ Cut the boring bits

You don't need "Hello, how are you?" — jump straight into the interesting conversation. Cut anything that doesn't move the story forward.

🤐 What they DON'T say

Sometimes the most powerful dialogue is what a character refuses to say. Silence, one-word answers, or changing the subject can say everything.

🎭 Every character is different

Box-Man shouldn't sound the same as his villain. Think about how each character talks differently — slang, formality, accent, catchphrases.

🎥 Production Planning

List all the locations you need. Think about whether they're available and if you need permission.

🎬 Shot List Builder

A shot list tells you exactly what shots you need to film. Fill one in for each scene.

🔭 Wide Shot (WS)

Shows the whole scene — where we are and who's in it. Good for establishing a location.

👤 Mid Shot (MS)

Waist up. Good for conversations and action where you want to see body language.

😮 Close-Up (CU)

Face or object fills the frame. Brilliant for reactions, emotions, or important props.

🏃 Tracking Shot

Camera moves with the character. Great for action sequences — follow Box-Man as he runs!
