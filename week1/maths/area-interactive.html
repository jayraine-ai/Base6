🟦 What is Area? ▼
Area is how much surface a shape covers — like how much space a rug takes up on a floor.
  • 1
    Area tells us how much surface a shape covers.
  • 2
    If a shape is on a square grid, you can find the area by counting the squares.
  • 3
    If each square has sides 1 cm long, each square equals 1 cm².
  • 4
    Area is always in squared units — like cm², m², or mm².

There are 6 squares and each is 1 cm². So the area is 6 cm².

✖️ Finding Area by Multiplying ▼
For a rectangle, there's a shortcut — you don't have to count every square!
Area = Length × Width Multiply the two side lengths together
✨ Example 1 — A rectangle
  • 1
    Area = 12 m × 5 m
  • 2
    Area = 60 m²
✨ Example 2 — Sophie's carpet

Sophie is buying carpet for her dining room. The room is 6 m long and 5 m wide. What area of carpet does she need?

  • 1
    Area = 6 m × 5 m
  • 2
    Area = 30 m²
Drawing a quick sketch of the room can really help — even a rough rectangle with the measurements written on the sides!
✂️ When Shapes Aren't Rectangles ▼
If a shape is an unusual L-shape, you can split it into rectangles, find each area, then add them together.
  • 1
    Split the shape into two rectangles.
  • 2
    Work out any missing side lengths.
  • 3
    Find the area of each rectangle.
  • 4
    Add the two areas together.
✨ Example — L-shaped room
  • 1
    Top rectangle (A): 3 × 3 = 9 m²
  • 2
    Bottom rectangle (B): 6 × 3 = 18 m²
  • 3
    Total: 9 + 18 = 27 m²
Look for a corner that sticks inward — draw a line across there to split the shape into two rectangles. There's sometimes more than one way to split it, and both will give the same final answer!
🔢 Using Area in Real-Life Calculations ▼
Sometimes finding area is just one step in a bigger problem — like working out how much paint to buy, or how many tiles you need.
✨ Example 1 — Paint tins

Thomas is painting two walls. Wall 1 is 7 m long and 3 m high. Wall 2 is 6.5 m long and 2 m high. Each tin of paint covers 12.5 m². How many tins does he need?

  • 1
    Area of Wall 1: 7 × 3 = 21 m²
  • 2
    Area of Wall 2: 6.5 × 2 = 13 m²
  • 3
    Total area: 21 + 13 = 34 m²
  • 4
    Tins needed: 34 ÷ 12.5 = 2.72
  • 5
    Round up → Thomas needs 3 tins
2 tins wouldn't quite be enough — there'd be a bit of wall left unpainted! You always round up when buying materials, so you have enough.
✨ Example 2 — Paving stones

Christine is laying a patio 4 m long and 3 m wide. Each paving stone is 0.5 m × 0.5 m. How many stones does she need?

  • 1
    Area of patio: 4 × 3 = 12 m²
  • 2
    Area of one stone: 0.5 × 0.5 = 0.25 m²
  • 3
    Stones needed: 12 ÷ 0.25 = 48 stones
