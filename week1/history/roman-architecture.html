🏛️ Ancient Roman Architecture

Why does Roman architecture matter?

The Romans were one of the greatest builders in history. Over 2,000 years ago they constructed roads, bridges, aqueducts and enormous public buildings — many of which are still standing today. Their engineering ideas spread across Europe and are still used in modern buildings. When you look at a domed roof, an arch, or a set of concrete columns, you are often looking at a Roman invention.

Key Roman structures

🏟️

The Colosseum

A huge oval amphitheatre in Rome, completed in 80 AD. It held up to 80,000 spectators and used concrete, arches and vaults to support the enormous weight.

🛕

The Pantheon

A temple with one of the world's first and largest unreinforced concrete domes — over 43 metres wide. Built around 125 AD, it is still intact today.

🌉

Aqueducts

Long bridge-like structures that carried fresh water from mountains into cities. They used a gentle slope and the arch to move water over huge distances.

🏛️

Roman Forum

The busy centre of Roman public life. Surrounded by temples, government buildings and monuments, all using columns, arches and marble cladding.

🛣️

Roman Roads

Over 400,000 km of roads were built across the empire. Built in layers — gravel, sand and stone — they were so well made that many are still used today.

🏊

Public Baths (Thermae)

Enormous bathing complexes with heated floors, pools and libraries. The underfloor heating system (hypocaust) circulated hot air beneath the floor.

Roman building techniques

Romans were the first to use concrete (opus caementicium) on a large scale — mixed with volcanic ash, it was stronger than stone. They perfected the arch, which spreads weight outwards rather than straight down, allowing much larger and heavier structures. They combined arches into vaults and domes. Rows of arches created the aqueduct. Stacked arches built the amphitheatre.
