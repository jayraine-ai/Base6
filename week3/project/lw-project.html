🎲 Director = Dungeon Master

🐉 You already know how to do this. When you run a D&D campaign, you don't script what the players say — you set up the encounter and they improvise inside it. Improvised filmmaking is EXACTLY that. You're not skipping the script; you're directing the way a DM runs a session.
🗂️ The encounter = the beat card

A DM knows what each encounter needs to achieve. A beat card says what each scene needs to achieve — one line. The actors find the words on camera.

🎭 The players = the actors

Before a take, give actors their goal in secret: "You want to get past him. You will NOT let him past." Conflict between goals creates the scene for you.

🎬 You're in good company

Loads of professional comedy is largely improvised from beat outlines — the funniest lines were never written down. Directors plan WHAT happens; actors discover HOW.

✂️ The edit writes the script

In improv filmmaking, the "script" gets written at the END — in the edit, when you choose the best takes. Filming first isn't cheating. It's the method.

🗂️ The Beat Board

📌 One card per scene. One line per card. Not what people SAY — just what must HAPPEN. If the card is done, the scene is ready to shoot. That's the whole prep.

💡 What a Good Beat Card Looks Like

✅ Good beat

"Box-Man discovers the stolen package and vows to get it back." — Clear job. Actors can improvise twenty different ways to get there.

❌ Too much

"Box-Man says 'Not on my watch' then walks left and picks up the..." — That's a script wearing a disguise. Let the take decide the details.

🎥 The Improv Shooting Kit

🎬 The three-take method

Shoot every beat three ways: Take 1 — play it straight. Take 2 — go bigger and sillier. Take 3 — try the opposite. The edit picks the winner.

🤝 The "yes, and" rule

Whatever an actor says, the other builds on it instead of blocking it. "Yes, AND..." keeps scenes moving; "no" kills them dead.

👀 The instant review

Watch every take back straight away, together. Keep or re-go in 30 seconds. This is where the film actually gets good.

📼 Roll early, roll long

Start recording before "action" and keep rolling after the scene ends. The accidental moments are often the best ones in the film.

📼 Take Log

After each filming session, log what you shot — and most importantly, the gold: lines or moments that came out of nowhere and HAVE to be in the film.
